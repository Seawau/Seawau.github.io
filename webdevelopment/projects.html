Research Assistant for HIT Lab

In the fall semester of 2025, I volunteered to work as a research assistant at the Health Intervention and Treatment Research Lab. They conduct clinical research to understand the reasons behind changes in health behavior, which often involves sleep and alcohol. When I started, I completed CITI training in Social Behavioral HSR and Social and Behavioral Research Best Practices for Clinical Research.

I was introduced to a wonderful team and a graduate student who mentored me. Throughout the 2025-2026 school year, I assisted with emailing participants for scheduling, payment, and their results. Other tasks involved scanning papers, packing devices and sending them out to participants, assisting with compiling and summarizing scholarly papers, and updating participant information, among others.

Working with the HIT Lab team was not only a wonderful experience, but I learned how to work with a small team. They were very responsive to answering my endless questions, offering advice while providing resources for grad school and additional research opportunities, and also assisting me in creating a research poster. They also offered to let me sit in on lab meetings and clinical interviews, of which I am still looking forward to.

Show Me Research Week Poster

During my time in the HIT Lab, my mentor and the PI assisted me in creating a poster for Show Me Research Week of April 2026. I designed my poster based on their examples, and used their guidance to comprehensively display my findings. Using data from a previous study of theirs, I looked at whether there were associations between prenatal cannabis use and depression. With a total sample of 210, 185 participants used cannabis during pregnancy, while 25 did not. I was surprised to find that depression symptoms were similar among those who did and did not use cannabis during pregnancy. One of the most important things aside from the data was also ensuring the poster was engaging and easy to read. At Show Me Research Week, I was required to give my spiel to intrigued attendees, as well as judges who scored my work.

If I continued a later study in this direction, I would like to conduct a longitudinal study that asks more questions about depression, and looks into the trimesters. The presentation during Show Me Week was also the most difficult part, but worth it. I’m leaning towards clinical work over research, but I find both vital, and presentation skills even more so. Following my path into clinical psychology and counseling, articulating my thoughts and communication skills will be particularly useful.

Course: Eating Disorders 2460

My junior year, I took a nutritional course on eating disorders. We were required to read the book Wasted, by Marya Hornbacher–it’s a graphic and potentially triggering read on her first-hand experience with eating disorders. Throughout this course, we summarized and analyzed her story, learned about different types of eating disorders, their history among men and women, and how media influences and facilitates them. We also learned, as many disorders seem to do, that eating disorders may co-occur alongside other disorders or develop into different types of eating disorders. Naturally, it also covered prevention techniques, interventions, and programs to address it.

The core of the course was the book, Wasted. It was a chilling, disturbing read that made me dread my next journal entry on it, but it was, as you might imagine, incredibly insightful. In the end, both the author and the reader conclude that for many disorders and mental struggles, there is not necessarily a point where you get completely better. It is much like my favorite quote from before, in which the accumulation of events in life lead to a change where nothing will be the same again. This struck me, not just the idea, but the realization that the experience of this author’s life held numerous, potential answers and perspectives I would not have discovered otherwise. This seems obvious, but I am spurred on to read and hear more stories so that I have a better chance at understanding these people who already struggle to understand themselves, and yet seem so much wiser than me.
